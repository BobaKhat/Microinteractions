Your friend has a point.

I actually think the best answer is not either/or. It’s both, but with hierarchy.

What I’d do

Keep your main portfolio as your main portfolio.

Then have:

your strongest case studies first

a separate section for Interactive Experiments, Motion Library, or Playground

and also build the bigger standalone public library long term

So:

Portfolio = curated proof
Library = living ecosystem

That way you get both benefits.

Why your friend is right

If a recruiter only sees your polished case studies, they may understand that you can think strategically.

But if they also see:

custom microinteractions

experimental motion

interactive prototypes

things you actually shipped

then you suddenly read as way more:

technical

inventive

hands-on

future-facing

And yeah, hardly any designers have that.

He’s especially right that these interactions can show a kind of raw creative range your case studies might not.

Case studies often show:

process

structure

decision-making

These experiments show:

taste

obsession

motion instincts

craft

That combo is strong.

Where I think you are right

You’re also right not to want the library to just feel like “portfolio part 2.”

Because your long-term idea is bigger than that. You want something more like:

a public reference library

inspiration archive

interactive collection

design/motion sandbox

That should have its own identity.

So I would not collapse the whole thing into your portfolio.

The smartest middle ground

On your portfolio, add a section like:

Interactive Experiments
A small curated set of maybe 4–8 of your best pieces.

Each one can:

open live

show a thumbnail or embedded preview

link out to the full library

Then separately, build the full site.

That gives recruiters a direct taste without requiring them to discover the library on their own.

Because that’s the real risk otherwise:
you build something amazing, but recruiters never click far enough to see it.

About deployment / shipping skills

You’re thinking about this the right way.

Knowing how to:

build something

deploy it

maintain it

organize it publicly

is absolutely useful.

Not because recruiters are obsessed with “Vercel” specifically, but because shipping signals:

initiative

execution

technical comfort

independence

That matters a lot now.

About tools

Your real goal is not “use every trendy tool.”
Your goal is:

use the minimum stack that lets you actually ship the thing well

That probably means:

Cursor / Claude Code for building help

Vercel for deployment

maybe Replit only if it genuinely helps prototyping

That’s enough. You do not need a whole tool Avengers lineup.

My honest recommendation

I’d structure it like this:

Main portfolio

case studies first

then a section called something like:

Interactive Library

Motion Playground

Experimental Interfaces

Show a few best interactions there.

Separate public site

full searchable browsable library

categories

tags

lots more experiments

its own branding

That way:

your portfolio stays recruiter-friendly

your library becomes a deeper world people can explore

and both strengthen each other

If I were advising you strategically

I would not hide this work from your portfolio.

That would probably undersell you.

But I also would not let it replace your portfolio structure.

So the move is:
feature it, don’t bury it, and don’t let it take over.

Your friend’s core point is right:
